Why I prefer to bottomfish with barbless hooks

One of the many reasons why I prefer to fish with barbless hooks is because of the amount of snag you will encounter while fishing on the bottom for rockfish, lingcod, or any other species of bottomfish. Usually, the bottomfish are plentiful enough that you do not need barbs to limit out. What is more likely to happen with barbed hooks though, is that you will lose more lures to snags to the point where you will spend more time retying lures than actually fishing.

What is a rockfish?

A rockfish can be from one out of many species of rockfish, or the family of fish known as Sebastes.

Lures for rockfish

Rockfish are vivacious eaters, they have poor eyesights and will generally attack anything they can see or smell. Lures and bait are great options for them with lures being preferred as many rockfish species have to be returned to the water alive, and using bait risks gut hooking these creatures which is almost a guaranteed death for them. Common species of rockfish in Puget Sound Depths Rarity Additional Notes
Black Rockfish Shallow, 10-120ft Very common, this species has very high fecundity and one of the few species of rockfish to withstand heavy fishing pressure Fishing for rockfish is closed entirely in Puget Sound but black rockfish are commonly caught in the Strait and the Pacific. Hard to fillet but they make for excellent fish tacos
Quillback Rockfish Shallow when juvenile, 50-200ft+ for adult Slow growing and long lived, these fish commonly live up to half a century. They are known for their mild poison in their spikes that will cause your hands to swell up painfully but otherwise non-harmful. Illegal to keep in most of Washington State. If you hook one, please carefully unhook without taking out of the water if possible. Every fish counts in rebuilding a population and we certainly need to all do our parts in conserving these majestic old beasts.
